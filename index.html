Design and Implementation of an Autonomous Surgical Robotic Aspirator

An autonomous robotic assistant for intraoperative blood aspiration in minimally invasive surgery, combining real-time bleeding segmentation, centroid-based target selection, artificial-potential-field navigation, and mixed-reality interface supervision.

Eva Góngora-Rodríguez, Irene Rivas-Blanco, Álvaro Galán-Cuenca, Carmen López-Casado, Isabel García-Morales, and Víctor F. Muñoz
Department of Systems Engineering and Automation, Universidad de Málaga, Andalucía Tech
Paper Code Videos BibTeX

Abstract

Robotic assistance in minimally invasive surgery has significantly improved precision and dexterity; however, many supportive tasks, such as blood aspiration, still rely on manual operation. This work presents the design and implementation of an autonomous robotic aspirator capable of detecting and removing intraoperative bleeding without continuous human intervention.

The proposed system integrates a perception module based on a convolutional neural network for real-time blood segmentation, a task planner for high-level actions execution, and a control strategy based on artificial potential fields for autonomous navigation. Additionally, a mixed-reality human–robot interaction interface is incorporated to enable system supervision and seamless transition to teleoperation when required.

Method

1. Perception

Endoscopic images are processed by a U-Net-based segmentation network to detect bleeding regions in real time. From the segmented mask, the system extracts region area, centroid location, and a temporal persistence descriptor encoding the age of blood pixels.

2. Decision-making

The task planner selects a target blood region, updates its 3D target location, commands navigation, and activates or deactivates suction depending on the distance between the tool tip and the selected target.

3. Autonomous Navigation

The local planner uses artificial potential fields to drive the suction tool toward the selected target while respecting remote-center-of-motion constraints. The current implementation primarily uses an attractive-field formulation and includes practical safeguards such as velocity saturation and target timeouts.

4. Human Supervision

A mixed-reality interface based on a VR headset displays the endoscopic view together with scene and system status information, allowing supervision and immediate switching to teleoperation with a haptic controller.

Centroid-Based Target Strategies

C1 — Geometric centroid

Uses the global spatial distribution of the bleeding region.

C2 — Source-oriented centroid

Prioritizes persistent pixels and tends to stay near the bleeding source.

C3 — Front-oriented centroid

Gives more weight to recent pixels and follows the bleeding front.

C4 — Deepest-point target

Selects the most interior point of the segmented bleeding region.

Bleeding Scenarios

S1

Source-centered accumulation: blood stays near the source in a near-horizontal configuration.

S2

Downstream flow accumulation: blood flows away from the source and accumulates distally.

S3

Bilateral flow distribution: blood spreads toward multiple accumulation regions.

Results

The bleeding segmentation model achieved strong pixel-wise prediction accuracy and stable threshold behavior, with a Dice coefficient of 0.686, IoU of 0.565, precision of 0.72, and recall of 0.73 on the independent test set. Qualitative results show that the system reliably captures the main bleeding regions under varying illumination, tissue appearance, and tool presence.

Geometric analysis: centroid strategies generate distinct target locations across scenarios. C1 and C3 remain consistently close in spatial distance, but that geometric similarity does not necessarily translate into equivalent suction behavior.
Scenario-dependent performance: no single target strategy is optimal in all cases. C2 and C4 perform best in source-centered bleeding, C4 dominates in downstream flow, and C1 is strongest in bilateral flow.
Trade-off: lower suction times and higher suction efficiency tend to be associated with lower final suctioned area, while longer executions often clear a larger fraction of residual blood.
Robustness: boxplots reveal meaningful differences in variability across strategies, especially under complex blood dynamics.
